# Ideology Label Academic Review

This review audits the ideology labels currently defined in `src/data/labels.ts`. Baseline definitions are drawn from political theory and intellectual-history sources rather than internet slang or partisan usage. Core references used: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entries on liberalism, socialism, anarchism, nationalism, conservatism, republicanism, democracy, cosmopolitanism, global democracy, and global justice; Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy on socialism and political philosophy; Oxford Research Encyclopedia on socialism and populism; Roger Griffin / Oxford-style scholarship on fascism as palingenetic ultranationalism; scholarly treatments of Hindutva, Zionism, Juche, and Islamic democracy. See source notes at the end.

Ratings: **Accurate**, **Mostly accurate**, **Needs narrowing**, **Problematic**, **Use cautiously**.

## Highest-priority fixes

1. **Rename or narrow labels that are too generic or polemical:** “Fascist-Authoritarian” → “Fascist / Palingenetic Ultranationalist”; “Theocrat” → “Theocracy” or “Theocratic Politics”; “Revolutionary Collectivist” → “Revolutionary State Socialist” if state power is intended.
2. **Split internally diverse labels before using them as precise results:** Zionism, Islamic Democracy, Accelerationism, Neoliberalism, and possibly Eco-Authoritarianism need narrower variants or explicit scope notes.
3. **Protect major tradition boundaries:** keep democratic socialism, social democracy, Marxism-Leninism, council communism, libertarian socialism, market socialism, anarcho-capitalism, and left-wing market anarchism separate; do not collapse them into generic “left,” “socialist,” or “libertarian.”
4. **Mark contested/noncanonical labels as experimental:** Cyberocracy, Techno-Anarchism, Neoreactionary, Panarchism, and Participism should be presented as niche or emerging rather than as canonical traditions.
5. **Avoid polemical use of abused terms:** “neoliberalism,” “populism,” “fascism,” “eco-fascism,” and “Hindutva” should be defined analytically and not used as generic negative labels.
6. **Add source-backed scope notes to the UI or data model:** add an optional `academicUsageNote` / `commonUsageNote` / `rhetoricalUsageNote` field if these labels are shown to users.
7. **Do a centroid follow-up pass:** several names are defensible, but their centroid values should be checked against the narrowed academic definition after label naming is stabilized.

## Source notes

- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: liberalism, socialism, anarchism, nationalism, conservatism, republicanism, democracy, cosmopolitanism, global democracy, and global justice.
- Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy: socialism and political philosophy methodology.
- Oxford Research Encyclopedia / Oxford-style political science: socialism as a diverse anti-capitalist tradition and populism as a contested thin ideology, strategy, or representational claim.
- Fascism baseline: Roger Griffin’s palingenetic ultranationalism framework plus standard histories that emphasize ultranationalism, anti-liberalism, hierarchy, mass mobilization, and suppression of opposition.
- Hindutva baseline: scholarship distinguishing Hinduism as religion from Hindutva/Hindu nationalism as political ideology.
- Zionism baseline: scholarship defining Zionism as Jewish nationalism/self-determination in the Land of Israel while recognizing multiple internal variants.
- Juche baseline: Korean studies/international-relations scholarship treating Juche as DPRK state ideology of self-reliance, autonomy, military self-defense, and Kimist party-state legitimacy.
- Islamic democracy baseline: political-theory scholarship treating it as contested among electoral democracy in Muslim-majority contexts, Islamic constitutionalism, and Sharia-constrained democratic theory.
